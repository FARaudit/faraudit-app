# The 30-Day Window

## Why every founder gets the same letter from the IRS — and why most ignore it until it costs them seven figures

## The 5-second version

If you incorporated a C-Corp this year and you don't know what an 83(b) election is, you have at most 30 days from your founder stock purchase date to file one with the IRS. Miss it and you owe ordinary income tax on every dollar your equity appreciates between vesting events. For a startup that exits at $50M, that's a six- to seven-figure tax bill that disappears if you sent one piece of paper to the IRS via certified mail.

I just filed three of them. Here's what I learned.

---

## What 83(b) actually does

When you buy founder stock at par value (typically $0.0001/share), the IRS sees zero income because the purchase price equals the fair market value. No tax owed.

But if your stock has vesting (which it should — investors will require it), the default IRS treatment taxes you on the value of stock at each vesting event. Year 2 vest worth $1M? You owe ordinary income tax on $1M even though you can't sell the shares to pay the bill.

An 83(b) election flips this. You tell the IRS: "I'm voluntarily recognizing the income now, when the value is zero. Don't tax me at vesting." Future appreciation becomes long-term capital gains.

For a founder planning a 3-7 year exit, the difference between ordinary income (37% federal + state) and capital gains (20% federal + state) on the bulk of equity value is the difference between keeping your house and selling it.

---

## The deadline that matters

**30 days from the date of stock purchase.** Not the date of incorporation. Not the date of the board resolution. The date your stock purchase agreement is executed.

For founders using Stripe Atlas, Carta, or Clerky, the stock purchase happens at incorporation — Day 0. Your 30-day clock starts then.

The IRS does not care if:

- You didn't know about the rule
- Your attorney told you it would be filed and forgot
- Your CPA was on vacation
- You're a first-time founder

Miss the window, miss the election. Forever.

## Why I'm writing this

Because I've talked to four founders in the last 60 days who didn't file an 83(b) on their first company. They didn't know about it. Their attorneys "were going to handle it." Their CPAs assumed someone else did it.

Nobody did.

The damage is permanent. There is no late filing, no extension, no apology. The IRS form 83(b) cover letter literally states this is a one-shot deal.

If you're reading this and you incorporated within the last 30 days, stop reading and go check your filing status. Right now.

If you incorporated more than 30 days ago and never filed an 83(b), accept the loss. Don't let it happen to your second company.

If you're considering incorporating soon, use a service that files 83(b) elections automatically as part of incorporation. Atlas, Carta, and Clerky all do this. Don't roll your own.
